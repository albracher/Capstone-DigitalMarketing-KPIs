Data Glossary

Here’s an explanation of each column in the final dat_kpis dataset:

Original & Cleaned Location Columns

  • location: The original, raw location string as it appeared in the input data (e.g., “Orlando-Daytona Beach, Florida”, “Mobile, Alabama-Pensacola, Florida”).
  • city: The city part extracted directly after splitting the location string by the first comma. This might contain multiple city names or DMA names (e.g., “Orlando-Daytona Beach”, “Champaign & Springfield-Decatur”).
  • state: The cleaned and standardized state name, corrected for cross-state DMA entries (e.g., “Florida”, “Alabama”, “Tennessee”).
  • city_primary: The single, primary city name extracted from the city column, intended for geocoding and mapping (e.g., “Orlando”, “Champaign”, “Mobile”, “Los Angeles”).
  • state_raw: A temporary column holding the state information immediately after the initial separate split, before manual corrections were applied (e.g., “Florida”, “Alabama-Pensacola, Florida”).

Ad Details & Targeting Columns

  • audience_segment: Describes the target audience characteristics or online behavior used for showing the ad (e.g., “Online Behavior>United States>Finance>Financial Planning…”).
  • creative_size: The dimensions of the ad creative in pixels (e.g., “300x250”, “320x50”).
  • creative_messaging: The marketing message or call to action used in the ad (e.g., “Subscribe Today - 10% Discount”, “Sign up Now - 10% Off”).
  • app_url: The specific website or app where the ad impression occurred (e.g., “accuweather.com”, “cnn.com”).
  • exchange: The advertising technology platform that facilitated the ad serving (e.g., “Google Ad Manager”, “PubMatic”).
  • device_make: The type of device on which the ad was displayed (e.g., “Smartphone”, “Apple PC”, “Windows PC”).

Core Performance Metrics

  • impressions: The total number of times the ad was displayed.
  • clicks: The total number of times the ad was clicked.
  • viewable_impressions: The number of impressions where the ad met industry standards for actually being considered viewable by a user.
  • measurable_impressions: The number of impressions for which viewability could be technically measured.
  • total_conversions: The number of times a desired action (like a sign-up or purchase) was completed after a user clicked the ad.
  • gross_cost: The total amount spent for the ad impressions/clicks listed in that row.

Calculated KPIs

  • ctr (Click-Through Rate): \((\frac{\text{clicks}}{\text{impressions}})\). Shows the percentage of impressions that led to a click.
  • conv_rate (Conversion Rate/CVR): \((\frac{\text{total\_conversions}}{\text{clicks}})\). Shows the percentage of clicks that led to a conversion.
  • cpa (Cost Per Acquisition): \((\frac{\text{gross\_cost}}{\text{total\_conversions}})\). The average cost to acquire one conversion. (\(\text{NA}\) if conversions \(\text{= 0}\)).
  • cpc (Cost Per Click): \((\frac{\text{gross\_cost}}{\text{clicks}})\). The average cost for each click. (\(\text{NA}\) if clicks \(\text{= 0}\)).
  • ecpm (Effective Cost Per Mille/Thousand): \((\frac{\text{gross\_cost}}{\text{impressions}}) \times 1000\). The cost for one thousand impressions.
  • view_rate (Viewability Rate): \((\frac{\text{viewable\_impressions}}{\text{measurable\_impressions}})\). The percentage of measurable impressions that were actually viewable.
  • vcpm (Viewable Cost Per Mille/Thousand): \((\frac{\text{gross\_cost}}{\text{viewable\_impressions}}) \times 1000\). The cost for one thousand viewable impressions. (\(\text{NA}\) if viewable_impressions \(\text{= 0}\)).

Calculating KPIs Per City/Location

# let's figure out which cities spent money most efficiently (all kpis)

# first, group everything by our clean city name and state
location_all_kpis <- dat_kpis |>
  group_by(city_primary, state) |> # group by sets the stage for aggregation, adds grouping structure to the data frame so we can look at data
  # for each city/state combo, calc totals 
  summarise(
    # find total num, conversions
    total_conversions = sum(total_conversions),
    # total up cost for each location
    total_gross_cost = sum(gross_cost),
    # add total clicks for cvr and cpc calcs
    total_clicks = sum(clicks),
    # add impression metrics for branding kpis
    total_impressions = sum(impressions),
    total_measurable_impressions = sum(measurable_impressions),
    total_viewable_impressions = sum(viewable_impressions),
    
    # --- direct response kpis ---
    # calculate the overall cost per conversion (cpa) for this specific location  
    # lower cpa = cheaper to convert people 
    # we check if total_conversions is > 0 to avoid dividing by zero 
    avg_cpa = ifelse(total_conversions > 0, total_gross_cost / total_conversions, na_real_),
    
    # calculate overall conversion rate (cvr)
    avg_conv_rate = ifelse(total_clicks > 0, total_conversions / total_clicks, 0),
    # calculate overall cost per click (cpc)
    avg_cpc = ifelse(total_clicks > 0, total_gross_cost / total_clicks, na_real_),
    
    # --- branding kpis ---
    # calculate overall ctr
    avg_ctr = ifelse(total_impressions > 0, total_clicks / total_impressions, 0),
    # calculate overall viewability rate
    avg_view_rate = ifelse(total_measurable_impressions > 0, total_viewable_impressions / total_measurable_impressions, 0),
    # calculate overall vcpm (cost per thousand viewable impressions)
    avg_vcpm = ifelse(total_viewable_impressions > 0, (total_gross_cost / total_viewable_impressions) * 1000, na_real_),
    
    # ungroup after summarise
    .groups = 'drop'
  ) |>
  # only keep locations that had at least 5 conversions, average cpa is more reliable
  filter(total_conversions >= 5) |>
  # finally, sort the whole table by the average cpa
  arrange(desc(avg_cpa)) |> 
  mutate(
    avg_cpa = round(avg_cpa, 2),
    # round new kpis as well
    avg_conv_rate = round(avg_conv_rate, 4),
    avg_cpc = round(avg_cpc, 2),
    # round branding kpis
    avg_ctr = round(avg_ctr, 4),
    avg_view_rate = round(avg_view_rate, 4),
    avg_vcpm = round(avg_vcpm, 2)
  )

# group by/sort by avg_cpa but aggregate by city_primary and state
# group by defines categories within data based on variables
# aggregation calculates summary statistics across groups we've defined

Observations for “Identify which locations were the most cost effective”

Looking at pure average CPA per location:

Generally, we have a large range of numbers.

In terms of “pure” CPA, looking at performance metrics first we can see that Lafayette costs the least per acquisition, and conversion rate/cost per click is also pretty good in comparison to a lot of other places. Lafayette also has the lowest vCPM, so we know that, in terms of pure viewable cost per thousand, we’re doing great for cost effectiveness. In terms of high conversion rates, as well as low avg CPA and middling avg CPC, we see Ft. Wayne, Panama City, and Augusta, all of which have great numbers. Per acquisition, they’re relatively low; conversion rates are higher than the usual numbers. South Bend and Mobile are also interesting because they have great avg CPA/CVR numbers, but their CPCs are a lot higher than the other 20 most cost-effective locations. West Palm Beach is also awesome numbers wise, but the CPA is right in the middle, even if the CVR/CPC rates are better.

If we look at avg branding metrics per city/location, then things start to change.

Looking at average click-through rates:

Although cities like Champaign, Tampa, Grand Rapids, West Palm Beach, and Augusta have the highest click-through rates, this becomes a question of whether or not this is really worth it. Of those five cities, the only one that’s anywhere near the top 20 most cost-effective locations, by average CPA, is Augusta. Even then, Augusta is the 7th most cost-effective location, based on average CPA.

Some other interesting finds are Ft. Wayne, Nashville, and South Bend. They’re all in the top 15 locations by click-through rate, at the 11th, 12th, and 13th top locations by click-through rate (respectively) but also in the 2nd, 15th, and 12th most cost-effective locations by average CPA.

Looking at average viewability rates:

If we look at viewability rates, some cities that we’re seeing, once again, are Toledo, Nashville, Los Angeles, Augusta, Chicago, Miami, and Memphis. Each of these cities are, in terms of pure viewability rate, 1st, 4th, 5th, 6th, 8th, 11th, and 13th in terms of cities with the highest avg view rate. In terms of the most cost-effective cities by CPA, they rank 5th, 4th, 14th, 7th, 11th, 8th, and 13th, respectively.

So, we can see here that in terms of viewability rates and avg CPA, Toledo, Nashville, Augusta, Los Angeles, and Chicago are all great cities. Of note as well: Chicago had an insane number of total viewable impressions at around 14.1 million. Los Angeles also had a ton, at just about 6.8 million. In terms of the rest of the cities, Nashville had around 2.3 million, Toledo had 155,722, and Augusta had 106,963.

Something to keep in mind is that although Toledo had the highest average view rate, the number of total viewable impressions just wasn’t as high as Chicago/Los Angeles. Therefore, Toledo may have good general statistics but not a great number of total viewable impressions. As a sneak peek we can see that Toledo also has a low average vCPM, which would give off the impression it’s a great city to deploy ads to. Again, however, the number of total viewable impressions aren’t amazing, though.

Augusta was another “small city”: although average view rate was at 61.05%, the number of total viewable impressions was “only” 106,963.

On the other hand, Los Angeles and Chicago had around a 60% average view rate but had far more viewable impressions, in the millions. Even at being the 14th and 11th most cost-effective locations, they still brought in a very good amount of views.

To close out this discussion: although we need to talk about average vCPM, there is no “perfect” city, but rather a lot of “in between” cities in terms of cost vs. branding metrics.

Looking at average vCPM, or (v)iewable Cost Per Mille/Thousand:

So, let’s look at average vCPM (which should, in theory, should kind of go hand in hand with average CPA, right). We can think of vCPM as the cost of just… having our ads potentially seen, while avg CPA refers to the cost of “actual conversions”, in a sense.

Some particular standouts, once again, are Lafayette, Toledo, Chicago, Los Angeles, San Francisco, Ft. Wayne, Miami, Memphis, and Nashvile. I would say Shreveport is a standout, but given the context of the rest of what we’ve looked at so far, it’s not a standout, per se. Although average vCPM is low, so is the number of total viewable impressions.

Now that we’ve seen all the general “cost effective” metrics, we can start to break things down in full.

So, what cities/locations work across the board?

I think there’s three main general “tiers” of cities.

The “best all-around markets”:

  • disclaimer: when metrics mentioned, it’s usually mentioning average metric, not median/50th percentile (also i asked gemini to summarize a lot of this, but i wrote the stuff before this section)

  • Augusta is a great city, and appears on most of the “top” lists with a low(ish) avg CPA, high avg CVR, high CTR, high average view rate, and low vCPM.

    • Its main weakness is lower scale: Augusta only really had 106,963 total viewable impressions.

  • Ft. Wayne is another great city: it has low CPA, high CVR, relatively good engagement (11th by CTR), and it’s also pretty cheap for branding (nowhere to be found for top viewability rate, but relatively low vCPM).

    • Its main weakness is also scale (only 293,919 viewable impressions) and that the average view rate was quite low

  • Nashville was another great balanced contender, being cost-effective in CPA, relatively high engaging (12th in CTR), and a high average view rate (14th). Nashville was totally one of the larger cities, or at least, had a larger number of total viewable impressions at around 2.3 million total viewable impressions. It also had a relatively low vCPM, coming in at the 15th “cheapest” in terms of vCPM.

  • Lafayette was great in terms of pure average CPA and vCPM, coming in at number one.

    • Unfortunately, the total viewable impressions was somewhat low, and it didn’t come up in viewability rate, or average CTR.

    • Therefore, by nature, the actual view rate wasn’t great.

    • However, in terms of the entire dataset, in terms of a smaller market, it had the lowest CPA and lowest vCPM.

Scale vs. Efficiency

  • So, to start off: let’s talk about high volume, “good-enough cost”/major cities

    • Chicago and Los Angeles have lots of impressions, which makes sense because they are major cities. Both cities had lots of viewable impressions as well as viewability rates. Although their CPAs weren’t great (11th and 14th, respectively), their vCPMs were in the top 10. Therefore, we could consider both of them “high volume” and “good-enough cost”

  • Next, we have the low volume but great general stats cities

    • Toledo was a very “cheap” city in general, being 5th in CPA and 2nd in vCPM. However, Toledo didn’t have many viewable impressions, coming in at around 155,722. However, one weakness was that click through rate wasn’t great. Hence, Toledo had super low volume but great general stats

Branding Only

  • dawg i’m tired

  • Champaign, Tampa, and Grand Rapids have great CTRs but aren’t cost effective at all.

    • Hence, although they have good engagement the conversions aren’t great, and they’re expensive

“Diagnostic Cases”/Flawed Cases

  • South Bend and Mobile have great average CPA/CVR numbers but their CPCs are way higher

    • The ad marketplace is quite… expensive, cost per click is really bad compared to other places

    • So, even though the cost per acquisition and conversion rates are generally pretty good, it’s just so expensive to get clicks, this could be for a variety of reasons
